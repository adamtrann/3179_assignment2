Australia's Fiery Reckoning

Exploring the Path to Disaster and the Aftermath of the 2019/2020 Bushfires

In the lead-up to the 2019/2020 Megafire, Australia faced a series of extreme weather events that set the stage for one of the most devastating fire seasons in its history. The combination of unprecedented dryness and temperature spikes created a perfect storm, significantly increasing the risk of bushfires. This visualization seeks to explore the lead-up to, during, and after the 2019/2020 bushfires, highlighting how these extreme conditions, reflected in the data on rainfall and temperature deviations, contributed to the catastrophic natural disaster that resulted in unprecedented destruction of wildlife and habitats.

Setting the Stage: Record-Breaking Environmental Extremes in 2019

In 2019, Australia experienced one of the driest years on record, with annual rainfall plummeting to just 275.71 mm. This drastic reduction in precipitation marked the least annual rainfall recorded across the analyzed period. Alongside this, 2019 also recorded the highest annual mean temperature deviation of 1.50 degrees Celsius above the long-term average, further intensifying dry conditions. These extreme weather patterns fostered an environment primed for disaster, leading to the catastrophic bushfires that swept across Australia that season.

The Effects on Air Quaility

The air quality during the 2019/2020 Megafire crisis was alarming, especially in New South Wales, where monitoring stations recorded PM2.5 values significantly exceeding the safe average of 12.5 µg/m³. The suburb of Oakdale faced the worst pollution levels, with its PM 2.5 levels reaching 250.2 µg/m³ - more than 10 times the daily recommendation.

In Victoria, air quality was comparatively better but still above the recommended average of 12.5 µg/m³, with Footscray experiencing significant impacts. The fires not only devastated the landscape but also posed serious health risks to residents.
